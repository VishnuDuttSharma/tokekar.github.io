ECE 6504: Advanced Topics in Decision Making for Robotics

  • Instructor: Pratap Tokekar (lastname at vt.edu)
  • Lectures: Mon & Wed, 4:00 PM - 5:15 PM in McBryde Hall 321
Class announcements, discussions, assignments, and due dates will be posted on Canvas.

Description

This course will cover fundamental and state-of-the-art research topics in decision making for robotics. The first part of the course will consist of background lectures by the instructor. This will be followed by an in-depth study of recent research papers which will be led by the students. The students will also work on a research-oriented course project. The goal of the course is to understand and critique research papers, identify open problems, and initiate progress towards solving them.

Topics include:

  • Combinatorial planning: Traveling Salesperson Problem and Orienteering
  • Submodular optimization, mutual information, entropy, Gaussian processes
  • Information-theoretic path planning: Adaptive sampling, Multi-armed bandit problems, Optimal stopping problems
  • Adversarial planning: Min-max games, Cooperative games, Decentralized Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (Dec-POMDPs)
  • Reinforcement learning

This course is a seminar-style course featuring student-led discussions of recent research papers. See more information on paper presentations here. In order to make the course self-contained, I will give introductory lectures that cover background material.

We will have weekly assignments, a semester-long group project, and an individual term paper. The assignments will be a mix of writing and implementation exercises. You will also have to write reviews for papers covered in the class.

The students will also work in groups on a research-oriented course project. More information about the project is here.

Course Project

The project can be any of the following types:

  • Survey: An in-depth survey of a research area. The deliverable is a survey paper (between 8-10 pages).
  • Theoretical: Work on formulating a new research problem and/or solving an existing problem. It is perfectly okay if you cannot solve the problem in its entirety during the class, however, you must demonstrate sufficient effort and progress. The deliverable is a project report (between 8-10 pages).
  • Implementation: Implement an existing or new algorithm. This can be entirely in software or on hardware. The deliverable is a demo in the last week of classes and a report (6 pages).
All reports must follow the IEEE Transactions format.

Paper Reviews

You will be asked to submit tbd reviews, written individually, for tbd% each. Each review must contain:

  1. a concise, formal description of the problem addressed in the paper;
  2. a list of what you think are the main contributions of the paper over the state-of-the-art;
  3. a list of shortcomings/weaknesses of the paper; and
  4. a list of open problems and possible future work related to the paper.
The review can be at most 5 pages, 10pt.

Prerequisites

This is a seminar-style course that will feature a discussion of research papers. An ability to read and critique research papers is required.

There are no explicit prerequisites. However, students should be very comfortable in at least two of the following areas: Robot Motion Planning (ECE 4984); Data Structures and Algorithms (ECE 2574); Probability, Filtering, and Estimation (ECE 5605/5606); Computer Vision (ECE 5554); Machine Learning/AI (ECE 4424/4524/5424).

Prior background in robotics is helpful, but not required. We will aim to keep the material self-contained. I will cover the relevant background materials in lectures.
